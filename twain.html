Mark Twain

Mark Twain (1835–1910), born Samuel Langhorne Clemens, was an American writer, humorist, entrepreneur, publisher, and lecturer, widely known as the "father of American literature."

Twain’s works are celebrated for their humor, vivid storytelling, and sharp social criticism. His novels often explored themes of adventure, morality, and the complexities of American society.

His wit and keen observations of human nature made him one of the most beloved authors in the world.

Famous Works

  • The Adventures of Tom Sawyer (1876)
  • Adventures of Huckleberry Finn (1884)
  • Life on the Mississippi (1883)
  • The Prince and the Pauper (1881)
  • A Connecticut Yankee in King Arthur’s Court (1889)
